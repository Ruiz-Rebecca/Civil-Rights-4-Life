What does the 5th Amendment state?

In the Bill of rights, the 5th amendment is described as the following:

"No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury, except in cases arising in the land or naval forces, or in the Militia, when in actual service in time of War or public danger; nor shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb; nor shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself, nor be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law; nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation.."

What rights are protected under the 5th Amendment?

The 5 rights protected under the 5th Amendment are:

  1. Grand Jury Clause
  2. Double Jeopardy Clause
  3. Self-Incrimination Clause (Pleading the Fifth)
  4. Due Process Clause
  5. Takings Clause (Eminent Domain)



Grand Jury Clause

The Grand Jury Clause means the government can't put you on trial for a serious federal crime unless a group of citizens known as a grand jury agrees there's enough evidence to formally charge you with the crime.

A grand jury primarily protects citizens by acting as a shield against unfounded/oppressive criminal prosecutions.


Double Jeopardy Clause

Double jeopardy is protected under the 5th amendment and guarantees that a person that was proven innocent for a crime cannot be persecuted for the same crime a second time.

The Double Jeopardy Clause offers three main protections for criminal defendants:

  • Protecting a person from a second prosecution for the same offense after an acquittal (a judgment that a person is not guilty of the crime with which the person has been charged.), meaning the government cannot retry a defendant for the same crime even with new evidence.
  • Protection against a second prosecution for the same offense after a conviction. (This has an exception as a retrial may be given if an appeal is succesful. )
  • Protection against multiple punishments for the same offense.

Pleading the 5th

Refers to an individual’s decision to invoke their right against self-incrimination.

What is self-incrimation?

Self-incrimination is the intentional or unintentional act of providing information that will suggest your involvement in a crime, or expose you to criminal prosecution.


What does pleading the 5th guarantee?

This entails exercising an individual’s right to remain silent. At trial, an individual may “invoke the Fifth” by declining to testify in their own defense.


Due Process Clause

The due process clause ensures that everyone is allowed (the same)proper legal actions before being deprived of life, liberty, and property.

Eminent Domain

Eminent domain is the government's power to take private property for public use, requiring "just compensation" for the owner

(It does not include the power to take and transfer ownership of private property from one property owner to another private property owner without a valid public purpose.)
