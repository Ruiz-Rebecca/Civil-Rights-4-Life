Here are some ways that the civil rights explained in this website can be used in your daily life:

  • 1st Amendment

The 1st amendment gives rights such as:

  • Freedom of speech
  • Freedom of religion
  • Freedom of the press
  • Freedom to petition
  • Freedom to assemble

You can exercise these rights in your day to day life by:

  • You can freely post information on social media by acting as a watchdog to goverment.
  • You can wear expressive clothing
  • You can practice your religion or choose not to.
  • You can peacefully protest for causes that you are interested in.
  • You can sign petitions to support causes or make change in your community.
  • You can read a variety of news from several different sources.
  • You can create art (Music, paintings, literature, and more)
  • You can choose what to and what not to say.​

  • 2nd Amendment

The 2nd amendment guarantees the right to wield a firearm as self defense or in the case of a local militia.
In your day to day life, the 2nd amendment can be used by:

  • Understanding gun rights:Regulation of the use/wielding of a firearm greatly varies from local governemnt to local government, state government to state government, and federal government as a whole.
    Therefore it is imporatnt as a whole to understand the limitations of the 2nd amendment's firearms posession and regulation.
  • Self Defense: Becasue of the 2nd amendment, you are guaranteed the right to wield a firearm (gun) for self-defense. However, to legally own one you must be of age (21 or older).
  • Lawful Activites: Firearms can be used for hunting, sport shooting, and protecting property or livestock. However, these actions must be done in lawful manner.
  • Supporting advocacy efforts for gun rights: If you want to advocate for gun rights, then through voting or contacting representatives, you can make changes in legislature.

  • 3rd Amendment

The 3rd amendment prevent soldiers from being quartered in your home without consent.

Therefore, you can use your 3rd amendment right by:

  • Refusing to house soldiers during times of peace.
  • Assert your privacy rights: Example: If in the future, you own a house and soldiers ask to be quartered or reside in your during a time
    when there is no war, then you have the right to refuse and deny the soldiers the right to quartering in your house.
    However, if in wartime, Congress may pass laws to allow quartering during this time, howver it must be specific and legal.
  • Seek legal counsel if the government attempts forced housing.(This protects your home's privacy from military intrusion, supporting privacy principles.)

  • 4th Amendment

The Fourth Amendment protects people from unreasonable government searches and seizures.

You can exercise your 4th amendment rights by:

  • Politely refuse searches: If police don’t have a permit or reason to do so, refusing is allowed.
    However, if there is a permit or a known reason, you cannot refuse, as the search would be reasonable.​
    • Asking if you are free to leave (if there is no warrant/probable cause
    • Knowing when exceptions apply: Examples of this include plain view, evidence , or a lawful arrest.
    • Knowing that a search permit or a probable cause is needed to be searched: Searches and seizures can occur without a search permit in cars, schools and public spaces.

  • 5th Amendment

    The 5th amednment guarantees the following rights:

  • The right to a grand jury.
  • The right to double jeopardy.
  • The right to plead the 5th.
  • The right to due process.
  • The right to eminent domain.

You can exercise these rights in your day to day life by:

  • Refuse to testify in cases.
  • Use the phrase "I plead the Fifth" to remain silent.
  • Knowing that if the governemnt needs your property, you must be fairly paid. (Eminent domain)
  • Knowing that you have the right tl double jeopardy.

  • 6th Amendment

The 6th amendment gives the rights to:

  • Speedy and Public Trial
  • Impartial Jury
  • Informed of Accusation
  • Confront Witnesses
  • Compulsory Process
  • Assistance of Counsel

You can exercise these rights in your day to day life by:

  • Knowing that you have the right to legal counsel (lawyer) in civil cases.
  • Knowing that you have the right to be informed of your accusation.
  • Knowing that you have the right to confront your witnesses in a civil case.
  • Knowing that you have the right to an unbiased public trial.

  • 7th Amendment

The 7th amendment guarantees the right to:

  • The right to a jury trial in a civil trial.
  • The right for a case to be reexamined with specific legal jurisdiction.

Therefore, the 7th amendment can be exercised in your day to day life by:

  • Being able to file a civil lawsuit
  • Demanding a jury trial
  • By knowing that the civil case must have a value of at least $20. ($75,000 in modern day)
  • Knowing that the judge upholds Jury's Findings

    • 8th Amendment

    The 8th amendment guarantees that:

    • No excessive bail.
    • No excessive fines.
    • No cruel and unusual punishment.

    You can exercise your 8th amendment rights by:

    • Challenging Excessive Bail/Fines
    • Challenging unfair sentencing.
    • Filing legal motions
    • Understanding what is cruel and unusual punishment.

    • 9th Amendment

    The 9th amendment guarantees that not all rights mentioned in the first 8 amendments are the only rights guaranteed by the Constitution.

    The 9th amendment can be exercised in your day to day life by:

    • Knowing that your common sense rights are protected.
    • Knowing that you are guarantee other rights such as marriage, healthcare, housing, travelling, and more.

    • 10th Amendment

    The 10th amendment establishes Federalism, or the concept that the states and the federal govermnet share powers.
    Therefore, there is no guaranteed civil right from the 10th Amendment. However, knowing the concept of federalism allows you to understand what the federal government can/cannot do.

    • 14th Amendment

    The 14th amendment gives the following rights to the people:

    • Right to acitizenship
    • Right to priileges
    • Right to Due Process
    • Right to Equal Protection

    A person can use this knowlege in their daily life by:

    • assert your "Due Process" by demanding fair procedures (notice, hearing) before life, liberty, or property are taken
    • Challenging discriminatory laws, often by consulting a lawyer to challenge state actions in court
    • Being aware on how to become a citizen or know what rights come with being a citizen.
    • Knowing that there are certin liberties that cannot be violated without due process.
