What does the 7th Amendment state?

In the Bill of rights, the 7th amendment is described as the following:

"In Suits at common law, where the value in controversy shall exceed twenty dollars, the right of trial by jury shall be preserved, and no fact tried by a jury, shall be otherwise re-examined in any Court of the United States, than according to the rules of the common law."

What rights are protected under the 7th Amendment?

The 2 rights protected under the 7th Amendment are:

  1. Right to Jury Trial
  2. Re-examination Clause



Right to a jury trial

The right to a jury trial guarantees a jury in federal civil lawsuits involving disputes over money or property.

The right to a jury trial is preserved in cases where the "value of the controversy" is over $20.


Re-examination Clause

The Re-examination Clause is that if a fact is tried by a jury, then a federal judge cannot overturn or change the verdict.

This limits how federal courts can overturn or change jury findings to prevent judges from interfering with jury decisions, ensuring that ensures that facts determined by a jury are given importance.

The only way for the judge to change verdict is by using common law.(An example of this is starting a new case.)
