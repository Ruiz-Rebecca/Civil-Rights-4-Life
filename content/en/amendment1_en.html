What does the 1st Amendment state?

In the Bill of rights, the 1st amendment is described as the following:

"Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances."

What rights are protected under the 1st Amendment?

The 5 rights protected under the 1st Amendment are:

  1. Freedom of speech
  2. Freedom of religion
  3. Freedom of the press
  4. Freedom to petition
  5. Freedom to assemble



Freedom of Speech

The freedom of speech guarantees the following rights:

  • Not to speak in certain conditions.
  • The right to wear what they want. (ex: Black armbands)
  • The right to use certain offensive words and phrases to convey political messages.
  • The right to contribute money (under certain circumstances) to political campaigns.
  • The right to advertise commercial products and professional services (with some restrictions).
  • The right to engage in symbolic speech, (ex: Burning the flag in protest).

The freedom of speech does not guarantee the following rights:

  • The right to say things intended to cause immediate illegal acts
  • To make or distribute obscene (rude) materials.
  • To burn draft cards as an anti-war protest.
  • The right for students to print articles in a school newspaper over the objections of the school administration.
  • The right for students to speak rudely at a school-sponsored event.
  • The right for students to advocate illegal drug use at a school-sponsored event.

Freedom of religion

The freedom of religion can be divided into 2 clauses:

  • The Establishment Clause: the government can’t establish an official religion or promote religion over non-religion.
  • The Free Exercise Clause: the government can’t stop you from practicing your faith.

Therefore, the amendment allows for anyone to practice any religion without being persecuted or stopped; and that the government cannot impose any religion on a person.

The American Civil Liberties Union describes certain rights that the government

  • The government can accommodate religion. (Example: Religious holidays off)
  • The government cannot promote religion. (Example: no mandatory school prayers)
  • You can’t break the law and use religion as a shield. (Example: Robbing a bank and saying it's for your religion)

Freedom of the press

The freedom of the press allows people to publish media through the news, papers, and even online without government interferance.

So what is the press?

Press typically refers to publishers of information, ideas, etc. Press applies to any publisher of information, not just journalists. Types of press include newspapers, television shows, social media, or any other forms of news sources. Freedom of the press allows people to freely investigate and report information to the public.


What does freedom of the press guarantee>

Freedom of the press guarantees several rights for the people. These rights are and aren't limited to:

  • the right to gather, report, and publish information without government censorship or prior restraint
  • The right for media to act as a public watchdog
  • The right to investigate wrongdoing
  • The right to provide diverse viewpoints*
    • *Invasion of privacy, however isn't allowed

Freedom to petition

In the Bill of rights, the freedom to petition is the right for citizens to peacefully ask the government for change or a "redress of grievances" without fear of punishment.

Petition protects lobbying, whether done personally or through a lobbyist, a person or company hired to present views or requests to government officials. This freedom also protects the right to sue the government in court.

Lobbying is a form of advocacy that legally attempts to influence and/or understand policy decisions made by legislators, members of regulatory agencies in the executive branch, or in some cases judges. -Wikipedia


What does the freedom to petition guarantee?

  • The right to ask the government for changes, fixes, or redress of grievances without fear of punishment.
  • Writing letters
  • Signing petitions
  • Lobbying
  • Testifying at public meetings
  • Contacting representatives (email, phone)
  • filing lawsuits against the government

    • This allows citizens to influence policy across all government branches.


Freedom to assemble

The freedom to assemble is the fundamental right for people to gather peacefully to express, promote, and defend their ideas.

(The first amendment creates rights for the people to influence public policy)

*This section is shorter because it's the most self-explanatory

This right works alongside the freedom of speech and freedom of the press, since assembling with others involves free speech and/or the promotion of ideas.
